 HOW NASA BUILT ARTEMIS II'S FAULT-TOLERANT COMPUTER (6 MINUTE READ)
[7] 

 The changes from Apollo to Artemis represent a massive leap in
software complexity. Today's software manages every thermal valve and
power relay, making it a challenge to ensure the software remains
synchronized and valid while under a barrage of cosmic radiation. This
article discusses Orion's zero-tolerance architecture and how it
offers a preview of a future where mainstream computing can achieve
the same always-on resilience required for space. 

 CLINICAL TRIAL SHOWS GENE EDITING WORKS FOR Β-THALASSAEMIA, TOO (5
MINUTE READ) [8] 

 β-Thalassaemia, a disease closely related to sickle-cell anemia, is
caused by a large range of mutations, so it can't be fixed with a
single editing system. Researchers took an approach that involved
reactivating the fetal version of the gene, which has a higher
affinity for oxygen than normal hemoglobin. Editing the gene in
patient stem cells and transplanting them back resulted in the
patients' hemoglobin levels rising. All patients in the trial saw side
effects, but they met the trial's key success metric of going over six
months without needing a transfusion to control their disease.
