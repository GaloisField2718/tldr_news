 DESIGNING FOR PEOPLE WITH DEMENTIA (6 MINUTE READ) [7] 

 Dementia, a syndrome affecting 55 million people globally,
significantly impacts memory, thinking, and social interactions, with
numbers projected to reach 139 million by 2050. Designing for people
with dementia requires avoiding auto-moving content and
memory-dependent tasks while providing clear borders on buttons,
descriptive labels, and persistent information traces. Rather than
oversimplified interfaces, users need resilient, error-proof paths
with dignified UX that preserves their sense of independence despite
cognitive fluctuations. 

 AGAINST CLEVERNESS (11 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 As AI and other complex technologies spread, good design must focus
on resilience, transparency, and recovery rather than blaming
individuals or chasing peak performance. AI amplifies latent errors,
deskills humans, and fails unpredictably, so systems should limit
autonomy, preserve human accountability, define clear boundaries, and
be designed to fail safelyâ€”aiming not for smarter systems, but wiser
ones that understand their limits. 

 THE INVISIBLE WORK (8 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 The most important work in organizations is often invisible: the
coordination, clarification, and judgment that keep projects from
drifting into chaos but disappear once things run smoothly.
Recognition follows visible outputs and simple narratives, so this
quiet leadership is undervalued, making it crucial for people who do
it to work under leaders who can recognize and protect the work that
actually makes progress possible.

 DESIGNING TRUTH UI: BUILDING TRUST IN THE AGE OF AI-GENERATED CONTENT
(2 MINUTE READ) [14] 

 C2PA metadata standards allow content to carry provenance
information, but technical verification alone doesn't build user trust
without thoughtful interface design. Truth UI uses progressive
disclosure to make authenticity visible through simple indicators that
reveal deeper information on interaction, avoiding overwhelming users
with technical data. For provenance to work at scale, it must be
embedded into design systems with consistent visual language, making
trust a natural part of the interface rather than an imposed feature.
