 HAVE WE FORGOTTEN HOW TO DESIGN? (3 MINUTE READ) [7] 

 Waymo, despite its self-driving technology, pays DoorDash drivers to
close car doors manually left open by passengers instead of
implementing automated door systems that already exist. This reflects
a broader problem in tech: companies choose inefficient solutions and
overlook obvious design alternatives when rushing products to market
and prioritizing cost-cutting over user experience. The design
industry has shifted from solving problems efficiently to simply
following corporate directives that prioritize profits over reducing
customer friction. 

 EXPERIENCE STRATEGISTS APPROACH UX DIFFERENTLY (6 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 Experience strategists work at the executive level, focusing on
organizational priorities rather than just designing products like
other UX roles. They identify how improving customer and employee
experiences can solve executive challenges, then orchestrate solutions
that achieve strategic business goals. For example, one strategist
studied small business owners' payment delays and connected this
insight to executives' revenue concerns, creating strategic
opportunities that elevated UX beyond just "making things pretty." 

 ACCESSIBILITY TESTING TAKES MORE THAN A SCAN (8 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 Automated accessibility scanners can quickly identify basic issues on
webpages but typically detect only about 40% of problems and cannot
evaluate real usability, making them insufficient on their own.
Effective accessibility evaluation requires combining automated tools
with manual checks (for structure and semantics) and experiential
testing tools (that simulate disabilities) to ensure users can truly
understand, navigate, and interact with a site.

 WHY AI-GENERATED UX FEELS OFF: THE HUMAN GAP IN DESIGN (4 MINUTE
READ) [13] 

 AI-generated UX interfaces look polished at first, but feel wrong in
actual use because AI lacks the contextual judgment and human
intuition that professional designers rely on to create truly usable
experiences. While AI excels at pattern matching and visual hierarchy
rules, it cannot anticipate user hesitation, weigh competing
priorities, or make design decisions based on real user behavior
insights. The speed of AI generation actually creates problems, since
good UX requires deliberate slowness for testing, revision, and
consideration of constraints that make design options appropriate for
specific contexts. 

 NOBODY GETS PROMOTED FOR SIMPLICITY (6 MINUTE READ) [14] 

 Complexity often sells better than simplicity, despite simplicity
being a virtue that requires hard work to achieve. In many engineering
environments, employees who over-engineer solutions get promoted
because their complex work creates compelling narratives. This
incentive problem starts in interviews where complex system designs
impress more than straightforward solutions, and continues through
design reviews where engineers feel pressured to add unnecessary
abstractions and future-proofing.
