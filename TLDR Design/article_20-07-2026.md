 OPENAI TWEAKS CHAT ACCESS IN THE CHATGPT APP FOR MAC (1 MINUTE READ)
[4] 

 OpenAI has updated its redesigned ChatGPT app for Mac after
widespread criticism of its confusing interface, restoring easy access
to chat history and Projects. The app now features a clear toggle
between Chat and Work, while a sidebar menu lets users switch between
ChatGPT and Codex, making the standard chat experience much easier to
find again. Although the update fixes the biggest usability complaint,
the app still relies on a larger Electron-based framework and has a
more complex interface than before, leading some to question OpenAI's
focus on integrating new AI tools over maintaining a polished Mac
experience. 

 GOOGLE VIDS NOW LETS YOU STAR IN YOUR OWN AI VIDEOS (1 MINUTE READ)
[5] 

 Google Vids now lets users create personalized AI avatars from a
selfie and voice recording, tied to their Google account and invisibly
watermarked with SynthID. The update also brings the Gemini Omni model
to Vids, enabling video creation from prompts and reference images,
background/lighting fixes, and step-by-step edits without restarting.
These additions expand Vids from a workplace presentation tool into a
broader video platform, positioning it against avatar competitors like
HeyGen and Synthesia. Access is restricted to users 18+ in select
regions. 

 BRING CODE-BACKED SCREENS ONTO THE CANVAS WITH VARIABLES ATTACHED (1
MINUTE READ) [6] 

 Figma now binds colors, type, and spacing to existing file variables
when code-backed screens are brought onto the canvas, rather than
importing them as hardcoded values. This applies to Make preview
copies, code-to-canvas generation via the Figma MCP server, and live
web pages captured through the Chrome extension. Frames also arrive
with auto layout more often, letting edits resize and reflow so teams
can refine designs instead of rebuilding them.

 META'S "REGRET" OVER ITS SCRAPPED AI TOOL IS LIKE A BURGLAR
APOLOGISING FOR THE MESS (7 MINUTE READ) [7] 

 Meta briefly introduced a feature in its new Muse Image AI tool that
allowed users to generate and edit images using photos from any public
Instagram account without prior consent, with the feature enabled by
default. After widespread criticism from creative professionals,
unions, talent agencies, and privacy advocates, the company removed
the feature within three days, acknowledging that it had "missed the
mark." The incident highlights a broader concern about AI companies
launching opt-out features that treat publicly shared content as
available for AI use unless users explicitly disable it. It also
underscores the importance of consent, creator rights, and vigilance,
as platforms continue to push AI capabilities that can affect
photographers, designers, illustrators, and other creative
professionals. 

 WHY IS AI BAD AT DESIGN? (7 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 AI struggles with design because, unlike code, design has no
automatic way to verify if an output is correct, forcing reliance on
slow, subjective human judgment. This subjectivity pulls AI toward
generic, "average" outputs, worsened by scarce design training data
and models generating interfaces blindly, without truly seeing what
they create. While vision-based feedback loops and better models may
narrow some gaps, the subjective nature of taste remains a structural
limit, meaning humans will still be essential for genuine design
judgment and originality.

 DOES YOUR FORM REALLY NEED A DROPDOWN LIST? (15 MINUTE READ) [12] 

 Dropdown lists are often overused because they save space and enforce
valid input, but they also hide options, increase interaction cost,
create accessibility issues, and frequently force users into
inaccurate choices. In many cases, radio buttons (for a few options),
comboboxes (for many options), or text inputs (for familiar values
like dates or ages) provide a faster and more usable experience.
Dropdowns are most effective only in a narrow set of situations: when
there are roughly 5–10 predefined options, the field is secondary to
the user's main task, or it needs to remain compact as part of a
larger group of related controls. Rather than treating dropdowns as
the default, designers should choose the input control that minimizes
user effort while matching the task and the nature of the data. 

 DESIGN SYSTEMS NEED EVALS (6 MINUTE READ) [13] 

 Design systems have adopted software engineering practices, but
agents generating code against these systems still can't be verified
for compliance without dedicated evaluation suites. Effective evals
combine mechanical checks—validating props, tokens, and component
usage against the actual system—with LLM-based judgment on pattern
selection and edge-case handling, run repeatedly since underlying
models and rules keep shifting. While a few teams like Meta's Astryx
and Intuit have begun building these regression suites into their
pipelines, running such evals in CI remains an uncommon practice
rather than a standard expectation.
