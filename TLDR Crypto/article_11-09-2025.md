GUIDES & TUTORIALS

 MARKETS NEED RULES, AND CRYPTO IS NO DIFFERENT (9 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 Crypto markets require clear regulatory frameworks to achieve broad
adoption, countering both the "regulatory free-for-all" approach
favored by some crypto advocates and the current hostile regulatory
environment. There are four essential objectives for crypto
regulation: predictability and stability, protection of property
rights, transparency and information clarity, and fair competition.
The SEC's approach of pursuing enforcement actions without first
establishing clear legal standards creates uncertainty that stifles
innovation while allowing bad actors to operate in regulatory grey
zones. Crypto-specific regulation should recognize blockchain
technology as both a financial system and a web infrastructure
platform, requiring specialized frameworks like token taxonomy,
decentralization standards, and guidelines that enable legitimate
businesses to operate without fear of arbitrary prosecution. 

 THE BOROS FUNDING RATE TRADE (7 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 Boros is a new Pendle-built platform that enables direct trading of
funding rates rather than underlying assets or perpetual contracts.
The strategy, called "Optimistic Funding Rate Trading," involves
longing rates when Binance's real-time funding rate rises above the
market's implied rate and shorting when it falls below, attempting to
capture both settlement gains and price movements from rate shifts.
The approach relies on timing market sentiment cycles in funding
rates, with traders earning the spread between actual funding
settlements and their locked-in fixed rate, plus potential profits
from correctly anticipating rate direction changes. This is high-risk
in nature, and there is potential for significant losses from mistimed
entries or misjudged market shifts.

 FROM WORLD COMPUTER TO GUARANTEE MACHINE (11 MINUTE READ) [10] 

 A developer building a real-time fitness aggregator demonstrates why
blockchains are fundamentally unsuitable for most applications by
detailing how a simple Python app becomes impossible to implement
onchain. The app easily handles continuous data streams, event-driven
updates, external API calls, real-time processing, and hot deployments
using traditional web infrastructure, but each capability is blocked
by blockchain constraints like determinism requirements, oracle
dependencies, gas costs, and sandboxed execution environments.
Blockchain applications remain toy-like because they're designed
around extreme blockchain limitations rather than actual user needs. A
new approach is needed to bridge the gap between computational
requirements and verifiable guarantees. 

 BASTION PROPOSES TO ISSUE HYPERLIQUID'S USDH STABLECOIN (3 MINUTE
READ) [11] 

 Bastion, a regulated stablecoin issuer with a New York trust charter
and broad MTL coverage, has submitted its proposal to issue
Hyperliquid's native USDH. Unlike other contenders, Bastion doesn't
run a competing chain or stablecoin, positioning itself as a neutral
infrastructure partner with strong U.S. regulatory compliance. The
proposal calls for USDH to be a full-stack stablecoin with segregated,
bankruptcy-remote reserves, 95% of yield reinvested into Hyperliquid
growth, and global on/off-ramp access via partners like Rain,
LayerZero, and Securitize. Bastion argues USDH should serve as the
base stablecoin for the ecosystem, with others built on top of it
rather than as wrappers.
