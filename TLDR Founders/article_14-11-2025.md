 WHY EVERY AI CODING TOOL GETS PRICING WRONG (17 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 How you price an AI IDE will depend on which subset of the market you
are going after, what your long-term strategy is, and how you
differentiate. Engineers will have large usage fluctuations, so
startups need to price accordingly to insulate against that. The
typical way of doing this is to charge users a subscription fee and
bill usage on top of that. Companies are now making price changes
several times a year just to keep up with shifting costs and adoption.


 THE COMPLETE GUIDE TO AI PROTOTYPING TOOLS (15 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 AI prototypes can turn speculation and pontification into evidence
fast. It's as simple as building four versions of a flow in Bolt or
Replit, pulling your real design system into Reforge Build, dogfooding
it the same afternoon, and seeing where people actually hesitate or
click through. The code is disposable, so there's no pretending it
speeds up shipping. This technique exposes which ideas survive contact
with reality.

MISCELLANEOUS

 WHEN CUSTOMER OBSESSION BACKFIRES: THE NETFLIX VS. BLACKBERRY LESSON
(7 MINUTE READ) [15] 

 Sometimes being customer-obsessed can work against you. BlackBerry
listened to feedback about wanting better keyboards and perfected
them, yet completely ignored the shift toward touchscreens that the
iPhone represented. History shows it failed spectacularly. The
difference comes down to the Einstellung effect, where expertise
creates blindness to better solutions. Founders need to distinguish
temporary customer truths like physical keyboards from permanent human
needs like efficient communication. This article offers frameworks,
including physics-first analysis and monthly anti-expertise audits,
where you argue against your strongest beliefs. 

 LENSES: WHY ONE VIEW IS NEVER ENOUGH IN PRODUCT WORK (5 MINUTE READ)
[16] 

 Product work requires juggling multiple perspectives at once. Anyone
who's opened a Miro board knows this truth. People create beautifully
messy diagrams with overlapping lenses because one view is never
enough to capture reality. This article breaks down nine different
lenses teams use simultaneously: customer journey flows, persona
mapping, Wardley Map axes showing capability evolution and user
proximity, team boundaries and interactions, opportunities and
options, and execution workflows.
