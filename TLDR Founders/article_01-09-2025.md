STRATEGIES & TACTICS

 DON'T LET CULTURE DRIFT AS YOU SCALE (4 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 Culture changes every time you hire someone new, but most founders
only notice when it's already broken - usually around employee 20 when
proximity to founders starts fading. The key isn't just defining
values but turning them into visible behaviors - celebrate when
someone lives them, call out when someone doesn't - because as the
military says, doing something below standard and saying nothing sets
a new standard. Culture drifts about every six months during rapid
growth, so plan regular resets at off-sites where you reconnect on
what values look like in practice.

 FLOUNDER MODE (3 MINUTE READ) [15] 

 Kevin Kelly has built clocks that tick for 10,000 years, co-founded
WIRED, wrote the "1,000 true fans" essay that launched the creator
economy, and advised Spielberg on Minority Report - but he's never
started a company and doesn't want to. He works "Hollywood style,"
jumping between projects that interest him, which Silicon Valley sees
as unserious compared to grinding toward unicorn status. While tech
culture fetishizes founders who get scurvy from eating only ramen or
"torture people into greatness," Kelly just seems happy, following
interests not for money or dominance but because the well of curiosity
is bottomless. The real question isn't whether you can be great
without suffering - it's why we insist greatness requires misery. 

 HOW COSTCO WON IN JAPAN (4 MINUTE READ) [16] 

 People waited eight hours to enter Costco's new Okinawa store - five
to park, three more to get inside - in a country where Walmart, Tesco,
and Carrefour all failed and fled. Costco succeeded by adapting
everything: building up instead of out with escalator ramps for carts
because land costs too much, selling 30% fresh food, including
restaurant-quality sushi at $20 for 48 pieces, and paying 60% above
minimum wage, which forced local shops to match or close. The real
genius was understanding Japanese shopping culture - customers split
bulk purchases with friends and neighbors, giving Costco 162,000
members per warehouse versus 112,000 in America, plus it let corner
stores resell Kirkland products during COVID at 20% markups because
spreading the brand mattered more than controlling distribution when
membership fees generate half your profit. 

 SURVIVING COVID, PMMC FIT, & SCALING TO $200M ARR (4 MINUTE READ)
[17] 

 Product-market fit isn't enough - you need PMMC fit, which means
having a product, market, profitable model, and channels that actually
work. Super.com nearly died during COVID when refunds exceeded
bookings. It burned money on TikTok ads, assuming viral content drove
bookings, but people searching "hotel tonight" use Google, not social
media. The same blindness almost killed its product - it thought
lower-income households wanted cheaper vacations. After making 200
employees cash paychecks at payday lenders and shops at Walmart, it
learned these customers needed everyday financial help, not travel
deals. It pivoted from discount hotels to a membership program for
credit building, grocery cashback, and basic financial services - now
it's at $200 million revenue.
