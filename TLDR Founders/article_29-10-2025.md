 MERCURY'S PATH TO PRODUCT-MARKET FIT (5 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 Mercury's story shows that product-market fit doesn't come from
iteration speed but from doing the hardest thing first. Before writing
a line of code, founder Immad Akhund spent months talking to founders,
lawyers, and investors to understand how fintech actually worked and
where it broke. That homework revealed the real barrier wasn't product
design but regulation and trust. So the team built slowly and
deliberately: a fully digital business account that could replace a
traditional bank from day one, not a lightweight fintech add-on. They
rebuilt the product twice, swapped out an underperforming partner
bank, and refused to launch until it worked end to end. When Mercury
finally went live, 1,500 companies signed up in the first week, one
depositing $1 million without ever speaking to the team. 

 A RESEARCH THINKING TOOLKIT TO SPEED UP THE DISCOVERY PHASE (26
MINUTE READ) [10] 

 Jeanette Mellinger urges founders to prioritize problem-solution fit
over speed, using a research toolkit adapted from UX practices. This
approach helps founders understand customer needs, behaviors, and
workflows, ensuring a product-market fit and a resilient team
foundation. Mellinger outlines three phases of research (Incubate,
Immerse, and Integrate), providing methods to master each element of
founder, problem, and solution fit, leading to sustainable and
effective growth strategies. 

 ASSESSING OVEREMPHASIZED STARTUP METRICS (4 MINUTE READ) [11] 

 Startups often inflate sales and partnership metrics, so investors
should check documentation for accuracy. Historical performance and
meaningful metrics, such as CAC and LTV, reveal execution capability
and business model efficiency. Patents are not enough in tech-driven
startups. Assess commercialization and pilot program outcomes to gauge
viability.

 DATA & AI INFRASTRUCTURE ARE FUSING (2 MINUTE READ) [17] 

 AI innovation requires a new data architecture focused on real-time
feedback, speed, and accuracy. Companies like Netflix and Stripe pivot
to unified systems with vector and context databases for rapid model
updates. This necessitates integrated observability layers to provide
comprehensive insight into data and AI health. 

 THOUGHTS ON THE AI BUILDOUT (4 MINUTE READ) [18] 

 AI growth is facing physical limits. Nvidia's profits could fund
several new TSMC fabs, which means chip demand now exceeds the cost of
producing them. The real constraints are power, materials, and labor.
The world needs more factories for turbines and transformers, and
hundreds of thousands of skilled workers to wire and cool datacenters.
Speed now matters more than efficiency, so natural gas is beating
nuclear and solar for new power. Hyperscalers are moving off-grid,
building modular datacenters, and paying suppliers more to accelerate
production. If growth continues, tens of gigawatts of new capacity
will come online each year. Even if the boom slows, it will leave
behind a massive industrial base ready to deliver cheap, rapid compute
for decades. 

 THE FUTURE OF THE WEB IS THE HISTORY OF YOUTUBE (5 MINUTE READ) [19] 

 Fifteen years ago, people making YouTube videos looked like hobbyists
in an already crowded world of content. In hindsight, the world was
actually short on video, waiting for tools that made creation easy.
Software is at that same point now. Large language models let anyone
with an idea build an app without writing code, turning small, niche
ideas into real products. This shift opens the long tail of software
that was never worth hiring a team to build. The people driving it
will look less like engineers and more like creators who understand
audiences and storytelling. The web is becoming more participatory
again, powered by people who make things because they can, not because
they have permission.
