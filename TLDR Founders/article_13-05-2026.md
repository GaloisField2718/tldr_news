 WHO'S WINNING ENTERPRISE AI NOW: CLAUDE UP 128%, GEMINI UP 48%,
OPENAI DOWN 8%, GROK STILL A ROUNDING ERROR (2 MINUTE READ) [5] 

 Enterprise adoption has shifted over the past twelve months. OpenAI
is still in the lead, but its share has dropped to 56%. Claude
adoption more than doubled in the last year. Gemini adoption grew from
27% to 40%, but Grok is barely growing within enterprise
organizations. The coding assistant market is driving the numbers. 

 HOW AI CHANGES SOFTWARE P&L (12 MINUTE READ) [6] 

 Saas used to mean 70% gross margins. AI-native software might cap at
17%. Every AI call is personalized, so caching and multi-tenancy don't
reduce your costs. Reasoning models burn 10-100x more tokens than the
ones they replaced, so cost per task barely moves even as per-token
prices drop. The math: 1 million users at $120 ARPU still leaves you
at 17% gross, 11% net. That's consumer goods territory, not
Salesforce. Four ways out: 1/10-cost clones (no moat), niche/lifestyle
businesses (now viable since build costs collapsed), luxury software
(Raya, Bloomberg Terminal), or vertical integration into atoms or
services. If your deck still assumes 80% margins because that's what
SaaS does, rewrite it this week.

STRATEGIES & TACTICS

 HOW TO BECOME "AI-NATIVE" (8 MINUTE READ) [7] 

 AI-native is an organizational label, not a tech one. It is estimated
that only roughly 1,000 companies above $5M ARR have actually rebuilt
themselves so agents can operate inside the core workflows rather than
at the edges. Most companies aren't legible to machines because
pricing logic lives in "Final_v7_NEW" spreadsheets, refund policy
hides in a Notion doc nobody trusts, and the sales process is "talk to
Sarah," so the AI-native shift requires making implicit knowledge
explicit: documented decision rules, agent-readable SOPs, clean
customer objects, permissions, audit trails, and evaluation loops.

 HOW WINNERS PIVOT (6 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 A pivot is any change that rewires your unit economics, not a
180-degree rebuild. Before you change anything, map your entire
conversion funnel in a FigJam, every marketing touchpoint, landing
page, demo, lifecycle, trial, and retentive user, with the conversion
percentage written between each step. The worst drop-off tells you
which lever to pivot. Then test the pivot MVP-style with five
different funnels for the five ICP groups you think will be stickiest,
each with its own creative, landing pages, and email sequences. The
worst mistake is broadcasting the pivot to the world before you have
retentive paying beta testers.

 AUTOMATE PROGRESSIVE ROLLOUTS WITH VERCEL FLAGS (1 MINUTE READ) [12] 

 Vercel Flags is a new tool for rolling out features to a growing
percentage of users on a schedule. It supports progressive rollouts
that gradually shift the traffic percentage to the new variant. Each
stage has a target percentage and a duration. Exposing changes in
stages allows developers to catch regressions on a small slice of
users before they hit everyone.

 PUBLIC STEALTH LEAVES OPPORTUNITY ON THE TABLE. WORKING IN SECRET IS
EVEN WORSE (6 MINUTE READ) [13] 

 Founders often harm their prospects by staying too secretive about
their efforts. Having no public trace of their startup work, such as a
domain or LinkedIn update, can cause them to miss out on critical
connections and opportunities. Basic visibility, like a personal
domain or relevant LinkedIn job listing, helps signal commitment and
legitimacy to potential investors and partners. 

 10X IS A LOT (3 MINUTE READ) [14] 

 There's a gap between claiming 10x and what 10x actually looks like
in the wild. Real 10x is the gap where the old way stops making sense,
email vs fax, Stripe vs wire transfers, not "23% faster but rounded
up." Run the test before you write "10x" in your hero copy and ask
whether your buyer would say the word unprompted on a call. If they
wouldn't, you're padding.
