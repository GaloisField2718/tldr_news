HEADLINES & TRENDS

 POWER, STATUS, AND LEGITIMACY IN TECH (20 MINUTE READ) [4]

 What made someone admirable in one era can look quite different the
next. Founders must continually reevaluate what earns legitimacy in
order to survive these cycles. One could opt to not play in this game,
but this would go against the industry's broad desire to change the
world. It would be bad for tech to be more culturally and socially
isolationist at the exact moment it gained the most power over culture
and society.

 THE PROBLEM WITH HYPERGROWTH AI STARTUPS (6 MINUTE READ) [5]

 With every AI startup seemingly hitting $100 million ARR in record
time, this article asks how much of that revenue is actually theirs. A
company can book $100 million and send $90 million straight to
Anthropic, OpenAI, or another model provider. Cheaper models do not
necessarily fix it. Customers will expect the savings or bring the
inference they already pay for. For your own startup, ask what they
would still pay for if the model were free. That is the part of the
product you own.

 THE OPEN-WEIGHT UNBUNDLING (15 MINUTE READ) [6]

 Kimi K3 is the latest in a series of open-weight models. Open-weight
models are estimated to be used in 80% of startups. They represent 25%
to 50% of the volume across major platforms like OpenRouter and
Vercel. The gap between frontier and open-weight performance has
compressed to 4 to 6 months and appears to be shrinking.

STRATEGIES & TACTICS

 VROOM POSTMORTEM: WHAT WE GOT WRONG ABOUT EVENTS (7 MINUTE READ) [7]

 VROOM started with a convincing idea. Event planners carry a list of
venues, vendors, and prices in their heads, so why not turn it into
software for a $90 billion market? Four months later, the team learned
that prices went stale, coordination was not that hard, and customers
were really paying for trust. They spoke to planners, but translated
trust into "information asymmetry." For your own startup, ask whether
the expert's advantage still works after that person leaves. If it
lives in relationships, you may be building a service.

 HOW TO USE LINKEDIN HIRING POSTS AS A B2B SALES CHANNEL (6 MINUTE
READ) [8]

 Everyone watches funding announcements for sales signals. By the time
you email the company, 30 other vendors are already mentioning its
Series A. A hiring post may be better. A founder advertising for an
SDR has just published the pipeline problem, approved budget, and
roughly three-month timeline in public. Search LinkedIn posts from the
past week for "we're hiring" and the role your product could replace
or support. Focus on founder-written posts from companies with fewer
than 200 employees, then show how you can produce part of the desired
result before the new hire even finishes ramping. You are not trying
to talk them out of hiring. You are arriving when the problem is
urgent and the budget already exists.

 Notion as code allows users to define and deploy an entire workspace
in TypeScript. Users can build workspaces with coding agents,
version-control the setup in git, and reproduce the same setup
anywhere required. They can review every change and keep a clear
history of how the work evolves in git. The same script can be used
across dev, staging, and production.

 Rogo Intelligence transforms firms' expertise into reusable
institutional memory. It sits between a firm's knowledge and the AI

and governable institutional memory. Rogo Intelligence transforms a
firm's intelligence into durable infrastructure that is governed,
permissioned, reusable, and continuously improving. This will allow

 WHY "A NICE PLACE TO WORK" DOESN'T JUST HAPPEN (6 MINUTE READ) [11]

 A new Zerodha employee knew the company does not push customers to
trade, but could not explain why. For a 16-year-old business whose
reputation rests on that decision, onboarding had preserved the rule
but lost the reason. The same culture shows up in smaller choices. No
3 AM emails, no weekend work, and no random founder ideas derailing
engineering. It can take almost two years for an employee to
contribute properly, so burning people out at year two is expensive.
Try asking a recent hire what your company refuses to do and why. If
they only know the slogan, onboarding fails.

 FORWARD DEPLOYMENT: THE ROLES, THE MYTHS AND THE LEGENDS (15 MINUTE
READ) [12]

 Everyone is hiring forward-deployed engineers now, but the title is
starting to mean almost anything. An actual deployment means flying
into a customer's operation, meeting everyone who touches the problem,
and shipping a first version in about a week. Deployment one is ugly.
By deployment ten, templates and integrations should turn most of the
work into configuration. If customer ten still needs the same people
and hours as customer one, you built a consultancy with a software
demo.

 INVISIBLE COMPANIES (14 MINUTE READ) [13]

 Steve Ross started by renting his father-in-law's unused funeral
limousines at night. He added parking lots, cleaning companies,
plumbing, and other fairly boring businesses. That collection
eventually bought Warner Bros. These companies were attractive because
ambitious people rarely bothered to look at them. Constellation
Software found a similar opportunity in tiny software businesses
serving marinas, funeral homes and libraries. There may now be more
than 38,000 of these vertical software companies. Perhaps for your own
startup, look one or two layers beneath the industry everyone is
chasing. The better business may be selling to the supplier nobody at
the conference thought was worth meeting.
