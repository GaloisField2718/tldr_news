HEADLINES & TRENDS

 SF IS BACK - AND THE TECH SOCIAL SCENE IS BEING RUN BY THE TERMINALLY
ONLINE (6 MINUTE READ) [5] 

 SF's tech social scene is driven by the "terminally online," with
connections and events organized through platforms like X and
Partiful. Traditional social proof is replaced by mutual online
interactions, impacting how relationships and trust are formed. This
shift suggests a potential broader trend where online social dynamics
shape real-world interactions globally. 

 THE SAAS ROUT OF 2026 IS EVEN WORSE THAN YOU THINK (5 MINUTE READ)
[6] 

 For the first time in the modern era, software stocks now trade at a
discount to the S&P 500. The iShares Software ETF (IGV) is down over
21% YTD and roughly 30% from its September 2025 peak, wiping out about
$2 trillion in market cap. The structural problem: AI is breaking the
link between headcount growth and revenue growth. If agents replace
seats rather than complement them, the per-seat recurring revenue
model that justified premium software valuations could unwind
entirely. 

 VENTURE CAPITAL HAS A STARTING LINE PROBLEM (7 MINUTE READ) [7] 

 Venture capital often favors well-networked founders who start with
advantages like connections and financial runway, leaving others at a
disadvantage from the start. This disparity isn't about in-room
investor bias, but rather the different starting lines shaped by
existing networks and resources. Underrepresented founders must
proactively build credibility within these ecosystems to secure
funding, despite systemic inequities.

STRATEGIES & TACTICS

 ANTHROPIC SEES THE MOAT. DO YOU? (7 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 Legacy enterprise software won by storing records. The next
generation of companies will win by building enough trust with
enterprises to work with the workflows where important actions
actually happen. Trust converts capability into permission to work in
enterprise. Anthropic is not the only company pursuing that position,
but it is one of the clearest examples of it. 

 HOW TO USE AI WITHOUT LOSING YOUR MIND (8 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 Most people are failing the basic test of whether their AI use cases
are even big enough to matter. This essay splits work into execution
(automate it) vs exploration (use AI as a thought partner, not an
answer machine) and argues that most people should ignore 90% of new
AI tools and focus on a small set of mature ones applied to their
hardest problems. Good antidote to the anxiety-driven "you must use
every new tool" narrative flooding tech Twitter. 

 ADVICE AND LESSONS FOR FOUNDERS (1 MINUTE READ) [10] 

 Online startup narratives often distort reality through selective
success stories and misleading exit optics that hide weak or
zero-return outcomes. Cofounder misalignment, role overlap, and
shifting contribution levels create common failure modes that surface
as resentment or functional redundancy. Recurring friction from legal
threats, employee apathy, and relationship-driven sales increases
founder workload and emotional strain.

MISCELLANEOUS

 ⚠️ WARNING FOR FOUNDERS: THE CURRENT DANGER ZONE IN VC (2 MINUTE
READ) [15] 

 The market can stay irrational longer than most founders can stay
solvent. Improving your financial profile should be your top priority.
It could take a couple of years. Either get profitable, or ensure that
you have enough funds to last for a very long time. 

 “BIG AI", AND THE VALUE OF A BUSINESS (6 MINUTE READ) [16] 

 Over 70% of mid-market B2B distribution businesses are expected to
change hands in the next decade. This monthly update from the CEO of a
B2B logistics software company explores how making operations more
systemized and "Amazon-good" can double a business's value before
exit. He also makes the case for "one to many" distribution through
channels and partnerships over traditional direct sales, arguing the
old SaaS playbook is losing steam. 

 DEMAND MIX, SHAPING, AND AI AS (DYS)FUNCTION MULTIPLIER (10 MINUTE
READ) [17] 

 AI will not fix broken product orgs. It will accelerate whatever
dynamics already exist. If your team treats engineering as a
bottleneck and optimizes around feeding it work, AI just speeds up
that dysfunction. If your team is oriented around learning and shaping
demand toward real outcomes, AI makes that more effective. Most orgs
never examine their "demand mix," meaning where work actually comes
from and how it gets shaped, and that gap is about to get a lot more
expensive.
