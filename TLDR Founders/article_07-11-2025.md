 VENTURE CAPITAL IS NOT COMPETITIVE (4 MINUTE READ) [5] 

 Venture capital no longer behaves like a competitive market. The
language of rivalry persists, but the substance has thinned. The same
firms crowd around the same deals, rehearsing the same convictions
until they harden into consensus. Somewhere along the way, the
infinite game became finite. What began as a search for outliers has
become an exercise in pattern recognition. Capital pools, performance
converges, and the illusion of competition masks a quiet retreat from
risk. The question is not who wins this round, but whether there's
still a game being played at all. 

 THE $100B QUESTION: HOW SAAS GIANTS ARE REWRITING THE RULES OF VALUE
WITH AI IN 2025 (10 MINUTE READ) [6] 

 SaaS pricing ran on predictability for two decades. Seats and flat
plans made sense when value scaled with people. AI broke that logic -
one agent can do the work of ten, and each query can carry a volatile
compute bill. Companies are now rebuilding their models in real time.
Hybrid pricing buys time but not clarity. Outcome pricing promises
fairness but demands proof. Between the two lies a growing realization
that software was predictable only because the people using it were.

plans include enterprise offerings, AI cloud services, and ventures

 THE LOST ART OF CRYING (5 MINUTE READ) [16] 

 Former Facebook VP Julie Zhuo just wrote the most vulnerable thing
you'll read about founder psychology this year. She reveals how
Silicon Valley's "ice queen" culture (the obsession with logic,
optimization, and emotional control) is actually a fear response, not
a superpower. As AI takes over rational work like calculations and
planning, the only irreplaceable human skill is our ability to truly
feel: grief, awe, connection, vulnerability. Zhuo discovered this
through what she calls "Big Cries" using music and solitude, and
argues that in an era where we're building superhuman machines,
learning to cry again isn't weakness but reclaiming the last thing AI
can never touch. 

 THE INTERNET WANTS TO BE FRAGMENTED (5 MINUTE READ) [17] 

 The social media empire is crumbling, and Gen Z is leading the
exodus. Nearly half of Gen Z plans to spend less time on social
networks in 2025, abandoning the "town square" platforms for intimate
Discord channels, DMs, and curated feeds. The big experiment of
throwing humanity into one digital room failed. This shift back to
small, self-selected communities is a massive opportunity for builders
creating tools for micro-communities instead of trying to be the next
Twitter.
