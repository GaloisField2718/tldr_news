 HOW TO COMPETE IN "CROWDED" AI MARKETS (4 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 Decagon CEO Jesse Zhang argues every AI market feels crowded because
only a few players can win, and walks through the brutal
customer-service AI sales cycle where Decagon gets compared to
Salesforce and Google adding AI, pre-AI Intercom, AI-natives like
Sierra, and the customer's own MVP. Speed of shipping is an advantage
but not a durable one, because AI tools have compressed build-and-copy
timelines so much that a feature lost in a deal goes straight onto
every competitor's roadmap. 

 AI CAN'T SURVIVE YOUR C-SUITE'S MAGIC THINKING (12 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 Corporate culture and agenda setting have failed engineering
organizations. Data wrangling and cleansing have become more about
making the data align with the business rather than making data more
valuable to improve decision-making. Executives are relying too much
on magic thinking. Shareholders and the board are now holding CEOs
accountable for choosing evidence over comfort to right the ship.

 The iconic stories of the Web 2.0 era give founders the hope that

 The winning move in AI now is to build a learning loop on top of the

 HOW RIVIAN PLANS FOR STUFF THAT DOESN'T EXIST YET (6 MINUTE READ)
[12] 

 Rivian needed a new factory for its cheaper SUV, so it built the car
in the old one and pocketed $2.25 billion. Its CFO's rule is to wring
every last drop out of what you already own before you pour concrete,
the hardware version of staffing for the work you have, not the work
you hope to get. The new model pulled profitability forward instead of
pushing it out, because sharing the factory spread fixed costs over
more cars and made the older lines cheaper too. Revenue tripled in two
years while operating costs held flat. Sometimes the cheapest way to
grow is to not build the new thing.

 DO YOU TREAT YOUR 20-YEAR CUSTOMERS WORSE THAN YOUR NEW ONES? YOU
PROBABLY DO (5 MINUTE READ) [17] 

 Lemkin names the "20-year tax" where loyal customers get squeezed
because they've already signed, integrated, and trained their teams

 THERE ARE MANY CONFIGURATIONS OF BUSINESS THAT WORK (12 MINUTE READ)
[18] 

 The venture-backed rocketship is just one way to build a company, and
you absorbed it without noticing. Patagonia stopped chasing growth,
let customers set its pace, and added a mission that cost real money,
and its near-death cash scares went away. Warren Buffett took the cash
off a dying textile mill's books, bought stocks with it, and grew
Berkshire into a giant without raising a dollar of venture money.
"Raise, grow fast, exit" is a script, not a law, and there are far
more ways to win than the one everyone copies. The startup is one
configuration. Is it the right one for the life you want?
