HEADLINES & TRENDS

 FINTECH'S MOATS DON'T COMPILE (4 MINUTE READ) [5] 

 Fintech's primary moats reside in regulatory permissions, credit
data, and risk-bearing capacity rather than replicable software code.
While AI compresses margins for pure SaaS by commoditizing code, it
strengthens fintechs that own their licenses and underwriting history.
Competitive advantage is shifting away from UI toward those who
control the "fin" side by managing money movement and proprietary risk
models that AI optimizes but cannot replace. 

 MANAGEMENT IN THE AGE OF AI (4 MINUTE READ) [6] 

 Managers must be builders in 2026 because not building now is the
bigger waste of time. They need to learn AI tools, and building is
often the most efficient path for the team. The ability to build will
be a key differentiator between teams that win and teams that drown in
their own output. The gap between good management and bad management
is now a lot wider. 

 BUILDING WHERE THE BUYERS ARE (5 MINUTE READ) [7] 

 For the last twenty years, the hardest part of building a tech
company was the development itself. AI is dissolving that constraint.
Companies no longer need to have large teams to ship products. This
means Silicon Valley may no longer be the center of the tech industry.
Companies needed to be near the engineers when the hard part was
writing code, but they will now move to where the buyers actually are.

STRATEGIES & TACTICS

 HOW YOU SOURCE GREAT DESIGNERS (6 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 If you want the best designers, you have to go where they are and be
someone they want to talk to. X is a good platform for designers who
are exploring startup opportunities. Look for designers who actively
follow tools and communities that signal taste. Most self-aware
designers move quietly through trusted networks. Founders need to be
plugged into those networks before they need them. 

 BEN HOROWITZ ON WHAT MAKES THE BEST FUNDRAISING PITCH (6 MINUTE READ)
[9] 

 The fundamentals haven't changed nearly as much as people think.
Strategy is still about articulating a clear 'why'. Product management
is still about shipping the right product at the right time. The best
fundraising pitch is the one you actually believe. The landscape
around the fundamentals has changed, but there are still massive new
opportunities hiding in plain sight. 

 THE NEW GTM PLAYBOOK: OUTBOUND ALONE WON'T CUT IT (3 MINUTE READ)
[10] 

 AI has commoditized cold outreach, forcing a shift from outbound-only
tactics to a multi-channel flywheel of content, community, and
retargeting. ColdIQ nearly doubled revenue by using content to prime
prospects, resulting in conversion rates 3x higher than traditional
cold contacts. Success in 2026 requires building this organic
distribution early, as the 6-12 month startup time creates a massive
lead over competitors still relying on saturated outbound channels. 

 HOW POSTHOG BUILT A MARKETING ENGINE THAT ACTUALLY DRIVES GROWTH (6
MINUTE READ) [11] 

 Tens of millions in ARR, $1.4B valuation, zero outbound sales calls
ever. Usage-based pricing with a generous free tier means one engineer
installs it for a side project and it spreads inside the company the
way Figma did. Their newsletter has 75K subscribers and never mentions
PostHog - just teaches engineers how to build better products.

MISCELLANEOUS

 STRATEGIC CHOICES: WHEN BOTH OPTIONS ARE GOOD (12 MINUTE READ) [15] 

 "Customer-first" isn't a strategy - nobody picks "customer-last."
Real strategy means choosing between two good options where smart
companies exist on both sides. "All-in-one" means you personally own
every feature request forever. "Infinitely extensible" means your UX
becomes a patchwork quilt. If the opposite of your strategy sounds
stupid, you haven't decided anything. 

 REVENUE ADDICTION KILLS COMPANIES (4 MINUTE READ) [16] 

 If revenue is your North Star, your company is destined to die.
Revenue-addicted companies kill every initiative that doesn't move the
number this quarter, which is exactly the opposite of what you need
during the AI transition when you have to make big product bets.
Lovable swapped its North Star to daily active apps and built a
qualitative "Lovable Score" combining NPS, PMF score, effort score,
and CSAT. Revenue still matters, but it's the output of the system,
not the thing you optimize for.
