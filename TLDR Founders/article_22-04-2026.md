HEADLINES & TRENDS

 THE NARROW PATH (6 MINUTE READ) [5] 

 Venture capital seed rounds have bifurcated into two distinct
markets, fundamentally breaking the traditional "spray and pray" fund
model that relied on 1,000x outlier returns. Top-tier founders with
legible traction now command massive entry valuations (frequently
topping $175 million) from brand-name firms, leaving a vast ocean of
low-priced, deeply adverse selection startups that operate as capital
black holes. 

 IT'S NOT JUST YOU. CUSTOMERS ARE ASKING FOR SHORTER AND SHORTER
CONTRACTS IN THE AGE OF AI (3 MINUTE READ) [6] 

 Enterprise buyers are increasingly demanding shorter contract terms
because the rapid evolution of artificial intelligence makes them
hesitant to lock into multi-year commitments. Recent data shows that
sales cycles have compressed while sub-one-year contracts have risen
significantly as customers try to keep their software options open. 

 YOUR AI FEATURE IS QUIETLY DESTROYING YOUR GROSS MARGIN (14 MINUTE
READ) [7] 

 AI needs to be treated like traditional SaaS COGS. The classic SaaS
model was built on high gross margins and low marginal costs. However,
add AI inference costs, model-routing costs, vector database costs,
and other AI infrastructure, and that margin profile can change fast.
AI can create a better product, but it can also quietly pressure the
most important leverage point in the SaaS financial model. Businesses
need to learn to isolate that pressure in their SaaS P&L, otherwise
they will feel it everywhere.

STRATEGIES & TACTICS

 GREAT COMPANIES ARE BUILT IN HACKATHONS (8 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 PostHog has shipped four real products out of hackathons. Logs became
one after someone ran the math on Datadog's bill and saw $260K a
month. The rules are what make it work. Nothing from the roadmap, no
sneaking regular work in, non-engineers participate. It treats
hackathons the way most companies treat roadmap planning, which is why
they actually produce companies. 

 THE RUMPELSTILTSKIN EFFECT: WHY WE BUY (3 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 Naming a problem gives you power over it. People feel better the
moment a vague struggle gets a clean label, even before anything's
actually fixed. Febreze coined "noseblind" and suddenly everyone was
panic-sniffing their couch cushions. Snickers named the feeling
"hangry." A consultant pitching generic marketing help loses, but the
same consultant selling a "Content Paralysis Crusher" package closes.
The companies that win aren't always the ones with the best product -
they're the ones who named the thing their customer was already
feeling but couldn't articulate.

MISCELLANEOUS

 NO DEFAULTS (3 MINUTE READ) [14] 

 No matter how obvious they appear, it's important to always question
all assumptions. In many cases, what is known to be 'true' is only
partially true, inaccurate, or simply the residue of a world that no
longer exists. It's important to reexamine and reunderwrite your
defaults from time to time. Carefully assess even those that seem to
be working - these are the most dangerous, as most will not think to
question them until it is too late. 

 STARTING A COMPANY AT THE BEGINNING OF THE EXPONENTIAL (4 MINUTE
READ) [15] 

 A month-by-month account of a founder watching his moat evaporate in
real time. November, two weeks of building replaces two months.
December, a week becomes a day. January, a day becomes hours.
February, a customer asks on a call why Claude can't just do the whole
thing and nobody on the team has an answer. The useful part isn't the
doom: it's noticing how quickly "we'll build it" stopped being a
defensible answer.
