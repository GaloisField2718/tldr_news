HEADLINES & TRENDS

 12-FACTOR COMPANIES (5 MINUTE READ) [7]

 Organizations shape technology and technology shapes organizations.
Each wave of technology changes what it means to build and run great
organizations, and AI is no different. The firm of the past was
designed around the bottleneck of human execution. The firm of the
future will be designed around the bottleneck of human taste and
judgment. This article looks at factors organizations can adopt to be
smaller, faster, and deliver outsized value to customers.

 2030-SHAPED SOFTWARE (7 MINUTE READ) [8]

 Everyone says agents will be the main users of software by 2030.
Okay, so what should the product actually look like? PostHog's answer
is pretty simple. Agents should do the work, while the interface helps
humans approve decisions and check what changed. If a person is
clicking through a screen only to perform an action, give the agent a
way to do it. Keep the screens where someone needs to judge the
result, resolve something ambiguous, or stop the agent before it does
something dumb.

STRATEGIES & TACTICS

 SPEEDRUNNING SOFTWARE (20 MINUTE READ) [9]

 Building an autonomous software factory feels the same as
speedrunning a game. AI made building cheap, and speeding up the
software factory lets you take more shots at building things. This
post looks at how Amplitude built its software factory, which helped
the company 3x its PRs. The faster a software factory is, the faster
you can put more ideas in front of customers to figure out what works.
They allow you to run more experiments and see good ideas surface much
faster.

 HOW COMPANIES ARE BUILDING AND SCALING THE FDE BENCH (7 MINUTE READ)
[10]

 Many leaders want a forward-deployed engineering (FDE) team, but few
know how to build one. The people who excel in the role are scarce,
expensive, and hard to interview for. This article looks at what an
FDE is, what makes a great FDE, how to find them, and more. It
includes an overview of how other companies are running the FDE model
and covers common misconceptions about the FDE bench.

 HOW TO FIND PROFITABLE PROBLEMS TO SOLVE (5 MINUTE READ) [11]

 Profitable product ideas often come from problems founders have
personally solved, giving them insight, proof, and credibility. Filter
those problems by links to money, time, or status, identify the root
cause, and validate whether buyers will pay for the transformation.

 WHAT I'D TELL MY TEAM ABOUT COMPETITION (5 MINUTE READ) [12]

 People love to say startups die by suicide, not competition. That is
wrong, especially in B2B. A lower win rate means less revenue, then
cuts, worse morale, and an even lower win rate. That is why he doesn't
care about 5% product improvements. A customer usually needs a
painfully obvious reason to switch, like half the price or 10x the
speed. Look at the deals you lost and ask what would have made the
choice obvious without another sales call.

 DOES TOPICAL AUTHORITY MATTER IN AI SEARCH? (9 MINUTE READ) [16]

 In a study of 1,094 US ChatGPT categories, only 15.2% had a clear
owner. Most companies are still fighting over categories where ChatGPT
has not settled on a default brand. Once a brand did become the
default answer, it stayed first in 90.4% of next-month comparisons.
Maybe don't publish the twentieth generic AI article. Pick the five
questions you want your company to be named for and build around
those.
