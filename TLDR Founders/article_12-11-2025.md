 THE FIRST $100,000,000 ARR AT DATADOG (5 MINUTE READ) [5] 

 It's hard to imagine what real customer focus looks like before a
company has revenue, but Datadog built it in from the start. The
founders spent six months interviewing users before writing code,
opened betas so customers could self-select, and sold month to month
to spot churn immediately. That approach scaled to 700 employees and
$100 million ARR. Engineers still rotate through support, pricing
talks double as product feedback, and leadership treats every
complaint as a signal. The result is a culture designed to find bad
news fast and fix it before it compounds.

 STARTUP OPERATING PRINCIPLES (2 MINUTE READ) [7] 

 Sam Gerstenzang grew from a fine to a good operator in three and a
half years of working at Stripe. This post shares how Stripe
approaches decision-making and communication. Operating well is a
state. Peak performance is a constant process of tweaking, iterating,

 STRATEGISM: MASTERING THE FOUNDER LETTER (7 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 In an era of formulaic marketing and AI-written content, the founder
letter stands out as one of the few tools that still feels human. It
works because it earns trust through story instead of shouting for
attention - naming a problem, stirring emotion, and only then offering
the solution. From Jason Fried's product launches to the plainspoken
clarity of Jeff Bezos and Warren Buffett, the format proves that
narrative still builds belief and conviction. Even small tests show it
moves results: pages using a founder's voice convert higher because
readers can tell when something was written by a person who actually
means it.

pitched the same company over hundreds of conversations, facing the
same objections and polite rejections. Then he closed $45 million from
12 funds. This post looks at what it really takes to raise a round.
It's a messy and non-linear process.

 THE COMPANY AS A MACHINE FOR DOING STUFF (10 MINUTE READ) [14] 

 Companies are machines for doing the stuff a founder really wants to
do, but bigger. The company is a vehicle to accomplish a goal that
would otherwise be impossible alone. Many founders have found wealth,
yet they reinvest it to reach further. Many people work for the fruit

 THE COMPOUNDING STARTUP (6 MINUTE READ) [15] 

 An analysis of 6,525 software companies revealed that outliers and
"normies" had nearly identical metrics at $1M ARR (the top 25% of
failures actually grew faster than half the eventual winners). The
difference emerged in relentless operational improvement: 86% of
outliers increased expansion revenue by 10%+, 72% boosted ARPA
significantly, and 51% improved retention - all while â€œnormiesâ€
stayed mostly flat. The compound effect is staggering as a 50%
improvement in pricing for new customers doubled annual growth rates
from 11% to 24%, while combining better pricing and retention took
growth "off the charts."
