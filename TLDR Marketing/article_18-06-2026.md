 NET INFLUENCER: THE GRWM INDEX Q1 2026 (15 MINUTE READ) [4] 

 LTK led share of voice in YouTube's GRWM videos, despite only
appearing in descriptions via affiliate links. The format is largely
unpaid, with nearly 90% of mentions driven by creator choice. Prestige
brands lost ground while Sephora gained share, and budget brands
surged. Rising platforms like ShopMy also point to increasing
competition in the creator monetization layer. 

 CHATGPT'S PRODUCT RECOMMENDATIONS CHANGE 80.2% WHEN SEARCH IS ENABLED
VS DISABLED (STUDY OF 20,000 RESPONSES) (3 MINUTE READ) [5] 

 ChatGPT's product recommendations depend heavily on whether search is
enabled, which means brands should focus less on influencing AI
training data and more on influencing the sources AI cites. In a study
of 20,000 responses, 80.2% of recommended products changed when search
was enabled, and only 15.8% of products that appeared in 100% of
non-search responses also appeared when search was enabled. The
findings suggest that cited web content plays a major role in
recommendation outcomes. Brands should focus on earning mentions in
the articles, reviews, and comparison pages that ChatGPT already cites
for high-value product queries.

STRATEGIES & TACTICS

 HOW PAID MEDIA ACCELERATES AI VISIBILITY (5 MINUTE READ) [6] 

 Paid media now plays a critical role in AI visibility because it
helps you identify winning messages and audience signals faster than
any other channel. High-performing ad copy should inform content,
community engagement, and thought leadership since the language that
drives clicks often becomes the language buyers recognize across
Reddit, YouTube, review sites, and other sources AI models cite. Paid
media should also fund distribution, since content that reaches and
influences audiences is more likely to generate the third-party
mentions and citations that shape AI answers. Treat paid, organic,
SEO, and community channels as a connected system where paid uncovers
insights, validates positioning, and accelerates the spread of content
that can compound visibility over time. 

 THE 95/5 PLAYBOOK FOR LINKEDIN GROWTH (4 MINUTE READ) [7] 

 Most B2B LinkedIn content fails at one of two extremes: too dense for
a social feed or too shallow to earn professional credibility. The
95/5 playbook addresses this by structuring content across three
complexity levels mapped to where a buyer sits in their decision
journey, then pairing it with two synchronized motions. Zero-click
thought leadership builds familiarity with the passive 95%, and
signal-triggered outbound converts the active 5%. High-intent signals
like gated downloads or pricing page visits earn a direct message
referencing the exact resource. Low-intent signals like likes and
comments get a softer reply first.

 STRIPO'S WIDGETS (3 MINUTE READ) [8] 

 Widgets let users create and edit interactive email elements through
chat instead of code. After adding a widget like a quiz or scratch
card, users can describe changes in plain language. The assistant
handles configuration, generates HTML, AMP, and fallbacks. It also
applies updates instantly, reducing the effort required to build and
maintain interactive emails. 

 SOUNDCORE WORK (1 MINUTE READ) [9] 

 Soundcore Work is a clip-on AI recorder designed for in-person
meetings. It records conversations, flags key moments with a tap, and
syncs transcripts to a phone in real time. Afterward, it generates a
one-page summary and lets users query the transcript for specific
details, reducing the need for manual note-taking.

 HINGE'S CHIEF MARKETER ON WHY COMMUNITY IS MORE THAN A MARKETING
CHANNEL (3 MINUTE READ) [10] 

 Hinge is building brand credibility with Gen Z by funding the social
infrastructure its users depend on rather than treating community as a
marketing channel. The ‘One More Hour' initiative has distributed
more than $2M in grants to grassroots social groups across New York,
Los Angeles, Atlanta, and London, with $1.5M more planned globally
this year, requiring no promotion or app downloads from recipients.
The strategy is a deliberate contrast to brands pulling back from
purpose-driven marketing, grounded in a real behavioral gap where Gen
Z spends 1,000 fewer hours of in-person connection per year than peers
did 20 years ago. 

 THE AI NOTETAKER WINNING BY NOT JOINING YOUR MEETING (3 MINUTE READ)
[11] 

 Granola grew by removing friction instead of adding features. It
avoided meeting bots and recorded audio at the operating system level,
which let it work across Zoom, Meet, Teams, phone calls, and other
audio sources without integrations. Its note taking workflow increased
trust by letting users create lightweight notes that AI expands into
useful summaries, which made the output feel personal and worth
revisiting. Granola also built habit loops through simple product
design such as one click meeting notifications that launch the call,
open the app, and start transcription. After earning trust as a
notetaker, it turned meeting data into a searchable knowledge base
through chat, shared team spaces, and reusable prompts, which expanded
its value beyond note taking and helped drive word of mouth growth,
70%+ weekly retention, and a $1.5B valuation. 

 PEOPLE PREFER PRODUCTS WITH FEWER INGREDIENTS (3 MINUTE READ) [12] 

 Consumers associate fewer ingredients with products that feel more
natural, healthier, and less processed, making ingredient simplicity a
strong messaging lever for food and beverage brands. Across 19
experiments, framing a product as having "few ingredients" increased
choice rates by 21.6% for an identical granola bar and generated up to
44.2% higher click-through rates in Meta ads. Brands should
prominently highlight low ingredient counts on packaging, ads, and
product pages, especially for health-focused products where
differentiation is difficult. The tactic is less effective for
products bought for indulgence, flavor variety, or nutritional
complexity, so brands should match the message to the purchase
motivation.
