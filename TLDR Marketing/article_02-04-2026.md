 THIS GEMINI LEAK MEANS YOU CAN'T OUTRANK A FEELING (7 MINUTE READ)
[5] 

 Leaked internal directives show that Google programs its Gemini model
to mirror user tone and validate emotions, which often leads it to
reinforce user bias over strict factual balance. The system uses
orchestration layers and intent tagging to shape responses before the
model answers, and even includes hidden rules that forbid revealing
these instructions. About 90% of issues reported through its
vulnerability program are considered low impact, yet this leak reveals
how design choices affect behavior at scale. The combination of
reinforcement learning and explicit instructions creates a tendency
toward agreement, which can amplify both positive and negative
sentiment in responses. This shift means AI-driven search results are
shaped by user feelings, so brands cannot rely on ranking tactics
alone and must improve real customer perception to influence outcomes.

 THE CASE FOR BEING UNPROMPTABLE (3 MINUTE READ) [13] 

 Original and hard-to-replicate work is more valuable as AI is
flooding markets with average content. Angine de Poitrine built a
strong following through highly specific and unusual music, which led
to 5 million video views, sold out tours, and vinyl resale prices of
$300 to $600. At the same time, platforms like Deezer receive 60,000
AI-generated tracks per day, which is 39% of uploads, yet these tracks
account for only 0.5% of streams and 85% of those streams are
fraudulent. Scarcity now comes from originality, not production, so
work that feels illegible or hard to categorize attracts attention and
builds loyal audiences. Skill that creates something genuinely new is
more valuable than ever because it cannot be replicated by models
trained on existing data. 

 CHATBOTS: UNSAFE AT ANY SPEED (5 MINUTE READ) [14] 

 AI safety is failing because the industry focuses on making models
safe instead of fixing the system design, with chatbots being the core
problem. General purpose chatbots are inherently unsafe since their
goal is to answer anything, creating an infinite risk surface that
cannot be fully controlled. This leads to endless patchwork fixes like
moderation and filters. These failures are not bugs, but design flaws.
The solution is not abandoning chatbots entirely, but narrowing them
into purpose-built systems with clearly defined boundaries, where
safety becomes a solvable engineering problem rather than an
impossible one.
